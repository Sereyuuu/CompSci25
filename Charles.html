BACKGROUND

Charles dicovered this law after hearing news of a balloon that was inflated by fire and travled a mile and one half. After hearing this, he attempted to replicate this with his own baloon and he found that the volume of gas was directly proportional to the temperature.

Charles' law states that volume is directly proportional to the temperature measured in Kelvin (K) as long as its at constant pressure. The law states that when temperature rises, volume rises; same when decresaed and vice versa.

FORMULA

NOTE: (After placing the given data, solve the problem using cross multiplication)

V₁ = Initial Volume, V₂ = Final Volume,

T₁ = Initial Temperature, and T₂ = Final Temperature


Transforming Celsius to Kelvin

Transforming Celsius to Kelvin is very important before you start solving using Charles' Law. This is to avoid any temperature that are in negative values hence why we don't use neither Celsius and Fahrenheit

To transform Celsius to Kelvin, we just need to add 273 to any number of Celsius. For an example, let's transform 45C° into Kelvin. 45 added by 273K is equal to 318K.

WORD PROBLEM #1

1.) A hot air balloon is filled with 3L of air at a temperature of 20°C. After some time, the balloon is heated to 60°C. What will the new volume of the balloon be?

Turning Celsius to Kelvin

Again, add the 273 to the value of the Celsius.

GIVEN & REQUIRED

V₁ = 3L

V₂ = ? (REQUIRED)

T₁ = 293K

T₂ = 333K

SOLUTION & ANSWER

Place the following given in the formula, then cross multiply. Then isolate V₂ by dividing 293K to both sides which also cancels out the Kelvin symbol (K). Now multiply 3L to 333, then divide it to 293 which will make our final answer to be 3.41L

The new volume of the balloon is 3.41L

SIGNIFICANCE

Learning about Charles' Law helps us to understand how gases behave when temperature changes. One good example is a hot air balloon. It explains how the volume inside of the balloon changes when temperature changes. This can help the person to control the temperature. Lessening the heat when they want to get off, and turning the heat on if they want a lift-off to the skies.
